1. Purpose

This policy establishes the organization's commitment to and requirements for maintaining business continuity, resilience, and disaster recovery capabilities. It ensures that the organization can continue to deliver critical business functions at an acceptable level during a disruptive event and recover to full operational capability within defined timeframes.

Disruptions may arise from many sources including natural disasters, power failures, cyber attacks (including ransomware), pandemic, hardware failures, supply chain failures, and human error. This policy ensures that the organization is prepared to respond effectively to any significant disruption regardless of its cause.

This policy aligns with ISO/IEC 27001:2022 controls A.5.29 (Information security during disruption) and A.5.30 (ICT readiness for business continuity), and complements the Information Security Incident Management Policy (POL-011).

2. Scope

This policy applies to:

  • All critical business functions and processes identified through the Business Impact Analysis (BIA), regardless of whether they are performed by internal staff or outsourced to third parties.
  • All IT systems, applications, infrastructure, and services that support critical business functions, including on-premises, cloud-hosted, and hybrid systems.
  • All organizational locations from which critical business functions are performed, including headquarters, branch offices, and remote work environments.
  • All staff with roles in the Business Continuity Plan (BCP) or IT Disaster Recovery (DR) Plan.
  • Critical suppliers and third-party service providers whose service disruption would materially affect the organization's ability to deliver critical functions (see also POL-010).

3. Business Impact Analysis

The Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is the foundation of the organization's continuity planning. A current BIA is a mandatory requirement of this policy.

3.1 BIA Requirements

  • A comprehensive BIA shall be conducted at least annually, and whenever a significant change occurs to the organization's structure, processes, systems, or services.
  • The BIA shall be conducted with active participation from business unit owners and senior management, under the coordination of the ISM or designated BCP coordinator.
  • The BIA shall identify: all critical business processes and functions; the IT systems, data, and resources required to support each function; the impact of disruption at defined time intervals (1 hour, 4 hours, 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 1 month); the financial, operational, regulatory, and reputational impact of each disruption scenario; and the dependencies between business functions and between systems.

3.2 Recovery Objectives

The BIA shall define Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) for each critical system:

  • Recovery Time Objective (RTO): The maximum time within which a system or process must be restored following a disruption to avoid unacceptable business impact.
  • Recovery Point Objective (RPO): The maximum period of data loss that is acceptable — i.e., how far back in time a data restore may go. RPO drives backup frequency requirements.

3.3 Criticality Tiers

TierLabelMax RTOMax RPOCharacteristics
1 Mission Critical 4 hours 1 hour Loss causes immediate, severe financial or regulatory impact. Customer-facing core services, payment processing, primary identity systems.
2 Business Critical 24 hours 4 hours Loss significantly disrupts operations but workarounds exist for short periods. Core internal systems: ERP, CRM, email, internal communications.
3 Important 72 hours 24 hours Loss causes inconvenience but business can operate with manual processes. Reporting systems, non-critical internal tools, development environments.
4 Non-Critical 1 week 24–48 hours Loss has minimal immediate impact. Can be restored after Tier 1–3 systems. Archives, historical reporting, training systems.

RTO and RPO targets for each specific system shall be documented in the BIA and reflected in the IT DR Plan's recovery procedures.

4. Business Continuity Planning

The organization shall maintain a documented, current Business Continuity Plan (BCP) that enables critical business functions to continue operating at an acceptable level during a disruption, regardless of whether IT systems are fully available.

4.1 BCP Components

The BCP must address the following as a minimum:

  • Activation Criteria: Clear criteria defining what constitutes a continuity event triggering BCP activation, and who has the authority to activate the BCP.
  • Command Structure: A defined Business Continuity Management Team (BCMT) with named individuals, their alternates, and clear roles and responsibilities during a continuity event.
  • Communication Plan: Internal and external communication procedures, including staff notification, customer communication, media handling, and regulatory notification.
  • Critical Function Procedures: Step-by-step procedures for continuing or operating each critical business function during disruption, including manual workarounds where IT systems are unavailable.
  • Resource Requirements: Identification of the minimum resources (personnel, equipment, facilities, data) required to operate each critical function.
  • Alternative Working Arrangements: Provisions for remote working, alternative office locations, or emergency workspaces if primary premises are unavailable.
  • Supplier and Partner Dependencies: Identification of critical supplier dependencies and escalation contacts for each.
  • Recovery Priorities: The order in which critical functions shall be restored, consistent with BIA criticality tiers.

4.2 BCP Ownership

Each critical business function's BCP procedures shall have a named owner (typically the business unit manager) responsible for: ensuring procedures are documented and current; ensuring all relevant staff in their function are trained on the procedures; and participating in BCP exercises. The overall BCP is owned by the ISM in conjunction with senior management.

5. Disaster Recovery

The IT Disaster Recovery (DR) Plan defines the procedures for recovering IT systems, applications, and data following a disruptive event that renders normal IT operations unavailable.

5.1 DR Plan Requirements

  • An IT DR Plan shall be maintained for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 systems. The DR Plan shall be aligned with the RTO and RPO targets defined in the BIA.
  • The DR Plan shall include: system recovery procedures (step-by-step, tested instructions for restoring each system from backup or failover); recovery sequencing (the order in which systems shall be recovered, based on criticality and dependencies); responsible personnel for each recovery procedure; contact details for vendors, cloud providers, and external support; and escalation procedures.
  • DR procedures shall be written in sufficient detail that a competent IT staff member who was not involved in creating the plan could execute them successfully.

5.2 Recovery Strategies

The following recovery strategies shall be evaluated and implemented as appropriate for each Tier 1 and Tier 2 system:

  • Hot Standby / Active-Active: Real-time replication with automatic failover. Near-zero RTO and RPO. Required for Tier 1 systems where technically feasible.
  • Warm Standby / Active-Passive: Replicated or regularly synchronized standby environment. Recovery requires manual failover. Suitable for Tier 1 and Tier 2 systems where active-active is not feasible.
  • Backup and Restore: Recovery from the most recent backup. RTO is determined by backup restore time. Minimum acceptable strategy for Tier 3 and Tier 4 systems.
  • Cloud-Based Recovery: Use of cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, GCP) as a recovery target. Cloud recovery environments shall be pre-configured, tested, and documented. Infrastructure-as-code shall be used to enable rapid, repeatable provisioning.

5.3 Failover Procedures

Failover procedures shall be documented, tested, and stored in a location accessible independently of the primary IT environment (e.g., printed copies in secure locations, cloud storage accessible from any device). Failover documentation shall be reviewed and updated whenever a significant change occurs to the system it covers.

6. Minimum Continuity Objectives

During a continuity event, not all systems and processes will be restored immediately. The following minimum acceptable service levels shall be maintained for the duration of a declared continuity event:

  • Customer-facing services: Core customer-facing services (Tier 1) must be operational or have a customer-visible workaround in place within the system's defined RTO. Customers must be proactively communicated with if services are degraded.
  • Internal communications: A working communication channel (email, telephony, or designated alternative) must remain available to all staff throughout any disruption. If primary email is unavailable, an alternative communication method (SMS, phone tree, messaging platform) shall be activated immediately.
  • Financial transactions: The ability to process critical financial transactions (payroll, customer payments, supplier payments) must be maintained or restored within defined RTO. Manual processing procedures shall be documented for scenarios where automated systems are unavailable.
  • Regulatory compliance: Compliance obligations (regulatory reporting, data protection controls, audit logging) must be maintained throughout any disruption to the extent feasible. Where a disruption makes compliance temporarily impractical, the circumstances shall be documented and regulators informed as required.
  • Security controls: As defined in Section 8, information security controls must be maintained during disruption. Any reduction in security posture during continuity operations must be documented, approved, and time-limited.

7. Testing and Exercising

BCP and DR plans that are not tested are unreliable. The organization shall conduct a structured program of continuity testing and exercises to validate plan effectiveness and staff readiness.

7.1 Test Types

  • Tabletop Exercise: A discussion-based exercise in which key personnel walk through a simulated disruption scenario. No live systems are affected. Focus is on decision-making, communication, and plan familiarity. Duration: typically 2–4 hours. Suitable for all participants including business leaders.
  • Functional / Simulation Exercise: A partial activation of the BCP or DR plan, testing specific components (e.g., IT failover, activation of the alternative site, communication cascade) without a full live system recovery. May involve actual switching of systems to the DR environment in a controlled window.
  • Full Interruption Test: A complete test of the DR plan by failing over all in-scope systems to the DR environment and verifying recovery within defined RTOs and RPOs. This is the most rigorous test type and typically requires a planned maintenance window. Performed at least annually for Tier 1 and Tier 2 systems.

7.2 Test Schedule

Test TypeFrequencyScope
Tabletop Exercise Semi-annual (every 6 months) All BCMT members; varies by scenario
Functional / Simulation Exercise Annual IT DR team; key business function owners
Full Interruption Test (IT DR) Annual All Tier 1 and Tier 2 systems; IT DR team
Communication Tree Test Annual (may be combined with tabletop) All BCMT members and alternates
Backup Restore Test Monthly (critical systems); quarterly (others) IT operations; selected systems per schedule

7.3 Test Documentation and Follow-up

All exercises and tests shall be documented. Documentation shall include: the date and type of exercise; participants; the scenario tested; outcomes against defined success criteria; issues identified; and corrective actions with owners and target dates. Issues identified during testing shall be tracked through the ISMS improvement process and resolved before the next scheduled test of the same type.

8. Information Security During Continuity

A disruption event does not suspend the organization's information security obligations. Security controls shall be maintained throughout any continuity or disaster recovery scenario. In some cases, disruption creates elevated security risk, and heightened vigilance is required.

  • Maintained Controls: Access controls, authentication requirements, data encryption, and audit logging must be maintained during continuity operations. Where primary controls are unavailable, compensating controls shall be activated as documented in the BCP and DR Plan.
  • Elevated Risk Awareness: During a disruption event, social engineering attacks (phishing, vishing) targeting distracted staff are more likely. An alert shall be sent to all staff at the point of BCP activation reminding them of increased social engineering risk and reinforcing reporting channels.
  • Out-of-Band Communication: If primary communication systems (email, internal messaging) are unavailable or suspected to be compromised, out-of-band communication channels (pre-agreed phone numbers, secondary email accounts, encrypted messaging apps) shall be used for all incident-related communications. Out-of-band channels shall be documented and tested annually.
  • Temporary Access Changes: Any temporary changes to access controls necessitated by continuity operations (e.g., emergency privilege elevation, bypass of standard approval processes) must be documented in the incident record, approved by the ISM or senior management, time-limited, and reversed as soon as normal operations resume.
  • Security Monitoring: Security monitoring (SIEM, endpoint protection) shall be maintained during disruption. If SIEM availability is affected, an alternative manual log review process shall be activated.
  • Documentation: All security decisions, deviations, and compensating controls applied during a continuity event shall be recorded contemporaneously in the incident log and reviewed in the post-incident review.

9. Alternative Processing Sites

The organization shall maintain the capability to continue critical business functions and IT operations at alternative locations if the primary site is unavailable.

9.1 Alternative Site Requirements

  • Warm Site: A secondary facility that is partially equipped and configured, capable of being activated within a defined timeframe (typically 4–24 hours) with some additional preparation. Warm sites shall maintain current copies of critical data, basic IT infrastructure, and network connectivity.
  • Cold Site: A facility with basic infrastructure (power, connectivity, physical space) but no pre-configured IT equipment. Cold sites may be appropriate for Tier 3 and Tier 4 systems where a longer recovery window is acceptable.
  • Cloud-Based Recovery: Cloud infrastructure shall serve as the primary alternative processing environment for Tier 1 and Tier 2 systems wherever feasible. Cloud recovery environments shall be maintained in a ready state using infrastructure-as-code, with automated provisioning scripts tested at least annually.
  • Alternative site details (location, access procedures, available resources) shall be documented in the DR Plan and known to all relevant IT personnel.
  • Remote working capabilities shall be provisioned and tested as a standard alternative working arrangement. All staff with critical roles must have verified access to required systems via approved remote access technology.

9.2 Alternative Site Security

Alternative processing sites shall implement equivalent information security controls to the primary site. Before activating an alternative site, a security readiness check shall be performed covering: network connectivity security (VPN, firewall rules); physical access controls; endpoint security of devices used at the site; and authentication requirements.

10. Dependencies and Suppliers

The organization's continuity capability is only as strong as that of its critical dependencies. Supplier continuity requirements are governed by the Supplier and Third-Party Security Policy (POL-010) and supplemented by the following requirements:

  • All Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers (as classified in POL-010) must maintain documented Business Continuity Plans and Disaster Recovery capabilities.
  • As part of the supplier due diligence and onboarding process, Tier 1 suppliers must provide evidence of their BCP/DR capabilities, including recent test results.
  • Tier 1 supplier RTO and RPO commitments must be equal to or better than the organization's own RTO/RPO requirements for the services they provide. This must be reflected in the Service Level Agreement (SLA) and Data Processing Agreement.
  • Tier 1 supplier BCPs and DR capabilities shall be reviewed annually as part of the supplier monitoring process.
  • Where a Tier 1 supplier's continuity capability is found to be materially inadequate, a remediation plan shall be agreed upon and tracked, and the risk shall be accepted by senior management pending remediation.
  • Critical supplier contact lists, escalation paths, and contractual notification requirements shall be documented in the organization's BCP and be accessible independently of primary systems.

11. Plan Maintenance

The BCP and IT DR Plan are living documents. Outdated plans are a significant continuity risk.

11.1 Annual Review

The BCP and all supporting DR plans shall be reviewed in their entirety at least annually by the ISM, BCMT, and relevant business unit owners. The review shall assess whether the plans remain current and fit for purpose, whether BIA outputs continue to reflect the business, and whether test findings have been incorporated.

11.2 Update Triggers

In addition to the annual review, the BCP and DR plans must be reviewed and updated within 30 days following any of these trigger events:

  • Significant change to the organization's structure, business processes, or service offerings
  • Addition, retirement, or major upgrade of a Tier 1 or Tier 2 system
  • Change of a critical supplier or material change to a supplier's services
  • Activation of the BCP in a real event
  • Identification of material gaps during a BCP/DR exercise or test
  • Significant change to the organization's physical premises or working arrangements
  • Changes in applicable regulatory requirements relating to continuity or resilience

11.3 Version Control

The BCP and DR plans shall be subject to formal version control. Each release shall be approved by the ISM and noted in the revision history. Superseded versions shall be retained for a minimum of 5 years. Current versions shall be stored in the organization's ISMS document repository and in at least one location accessible independently of the primary IT environment.

12. Training and Awareness

The effectiveness of the BCP and DR Plan depends on personnel understanding their roles and responsibilities. A structured training and awareness program is required.

12.1 General Awareness

  • All staff shall receive general business continuity awareness training as part of their induction program and as a refresher at least annually. Training shall cover: what business continuity is and why it matters; how to recognize a continuity event; how to report a potential continuity event; general safe working practices during disruption; and how to access BCP information if normal systems are unavailable.

12.2 Role-Specific Training

  • All members of the Business Continuity Management Team (BCMT) and IT DR team shall receive detailed training on the BCP and DR Plan relevant to their specific role. This training shall be completed within 30 days of joining the team and refreshed annually.
  • Training shall include hands-on participation in at least one tabletop exercise annually.
  • IT DR team members shall participate in at least one functional or full interruption test annually.
  • BCMT members shall be trained on: their authority during a declared continuity event; decision-making under uncertainty; escalation procedures; communication responsibilities; and their alternate's contact details and capability.

12.3 Training Records

Records of all BCP and DR training and exercise participation shall be maintained by the ISM or HR, including the date, type of training/exercise, and names of participants. Training records shall be retained for a minimum of 3 years.
