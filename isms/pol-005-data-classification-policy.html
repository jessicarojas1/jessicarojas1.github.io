1. Purpose

This Data Classification Policy establishes a consistent framework for classifying organizational information according to its sensitivity, value, and the potential impact of its unauthorized disclosure, modification, or loss. Classification ensures that appropriate and proportionate security controls are applied to protect information throughout its lifecycle, from creation through to disposal.

Without consistent classification, organizations risk either over-protecting low-sensitivity data (wasting resources) or under-protecting high-sensitivity data (creating unacceptable risk). This policy provides clear, actionable guidance that enables all personnel to identify how to handle information appropriately and confidently.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all organizational information in any format — electronic, physical, or verbal — regardless of the system, medium, or location in which it is stored or processed. It applies to all personnel who create, access, handle, transmit, store, or dispose of organizational information, including employees, contractors, vendors, and third parties.

This policy applies to information processed or stored on organizational premises, in cloud environments, on remote worker devices, and in any other location where organizational information is handled.

3. Classification Levels

The organization uses four classification levels. When in doubt about the appropriate classification for a piece of information, users should apply the higher classification level. Reclassification can be requested through the process described in Section 8.

3.1 Public

DescriptionInformation that has been formally approved for public release by an authorized individual (e.g., marketing materials, published press releases, public website content, published annual reports). Loss or unauthorized modification of Public information presents minimal risk to the organization.
ExamplesMarketing brochures, public website content, published case studies, job postings, publicly released financial reports, product documentation intended for customers
Handling RequirementsNo restrictions on distribution or access. Standard precautions against unauthorized modification should still be observed. Public information must still be accurate and authorized for release before publication.

3.2 Internal

DescriptionInformation intended for general use within the organization that is not approved for public release. Unauthorized disclosure of Internal information may cause minor reputational or operational impact, but does not present significant legal or financial risk.
ExamplesInternal policies and procedures, organizational charts, meeting minutes, internal memos, general project plans, internal training materials, staff directories, non-sensitive business correspondence
Handling RequirementsAccess restricted to authorized employees and contractors with a business need. Must not be shared externally without authorization. Basic access controls apply. May be stored on internal systems without encryption, but must not be stored on unapproved external or personal systems.

3.3 Confidential

DescriptionSensitive business information whose unauthorized disclosure could cause significant harm to the organization, its clients, or its partners, including financial loss, regulatory penalties, or competitive disadvantage. Access is restricted to individuals with an explicit need to know.
ExamplesFinancial statements (pre-release), business strategies and M&A plans, client contracts and commercial terms, employee performance data, salary information, non-public legal matters, security vulnerability assessments, supplier pricing agreements, audit reports
Handling RequirementsAccess restricted to named individuals or specific roles with explicit approval from the data owner. Encryption required for data in transit. Must be stored on approved, access-controlled systems. External sharing requires explicit written authorization and appropriate data sharing agreements. Physical documents must be stored in locked storage. Documents must be labeled "CONFIDENTIAL".

3.4 Restricted

DescriptionThe highest classification level. Information whose unauthorized disclosure, modification, or loss would cause severe harm to individuals, the organization, or third parties, including regulatory violations, legal liability, significant financial loss, or threat to personal safety. Access is strictly controlled and limited to the minimum number of individuals necessary.
ExamplesPersonally Identifiable Information (PII), Protected Health Information (PHI), Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), payment card data (PCI), authentication credentials (passwords, private keys, certificates), encryption key material, incident response plans containing sensitive vulnerability details, trade secrets, merger & acquisition due diligence materials
Handling RequirementsStrictly need-to-know access, individually authorized by the data owner and Information Security Manager. Encryption required for data both at rest and in transit. Data minimization principles apply — collect and retain only the minimum data necessary. Must not be transmitted via unencrypted channels. Removable media containing Restricted data must be encrypted and authorized. External sharing requires senior management approval and legally binding data protection agreements. Physical documents must be stored in locked, access-controlled storage and must be shredded when no longer needed. Documents must be labeled "RESTRICTED".

4. Classification Responsibilities

The responsibility for classifying information rests with the data owner — the individual or role with primary accountability for the information asset. Classification responsibilities are distributed as follows:

  • Data Owners are responsible for assigning and maintaining the classification of information assets under their ownership. They must classify information at the point of creation or acquisition and review classification when the information's content, purpose, or context changes materially.
  • All Users are responsible for handling information according to its assigned classification level. If a user creates information and is unsure of the appropriate classification, they must apply the next higher level and consult the data owner or Information Security Manager for confirmation.
  • Information Security Manager is responsible for maintaining this policy, providing classification guidance to staff, resolving classification disputes, and auditing compliance with classification and handling requirements.
  • IT Department is responsible for implementing technical controls that enforce classification-based handling requirements, including access controls, encryption enforcement, and data loss prevention (DLP) tooling where deployed.

5. Labeling and Marking

Information classified at the Internal level or above must be labeled to ensure that all recipients and handlers are aware of its classification and can apply appropriate handling controls. Labeling requirements by medium are as follows:

  • Documents (electronic and physical): The classification level must appear in the header and footer of every page. For short documents (one page), it must appear at the top of the document. Example: CONFIDENTIAL or RESTRICTED.
  • Email: The classification level of the email content must be indicated in the subject line as a prefix, using brackets. Example subject line: [CONFIDENTIAL] Q3 Financial Projections. Email classification is determined by the most sensitive piece of information contained in the email or any of its attachments.
  • Files and folders: File and folder names should include the classification level as a prefix or suffix where technically feasible. Example: Legal_NDA_Template_CONFIDENTIAL.docx. Folder structures for storing classified data should be clearly named to indicate the classification of their contents.
  • Systems and applications: Systems that process or store Confidential or Restricted data must be identified as such in the Asset Inventory. Login banners or splash screens must indicate the classification of information accessible through the system where appropriate.
  • Physical media (USB drives, portable hard drives, printed materials): Must be labeled with the classification level using a physical label. Restricted media must be individually tracked and inventoried.

Public information does not require classification labels. Internal information may optionally be labeled "INTERNAL USE ONLY" but this is not mandatory for all documents.

6. Data Handling Requirements

The following table summarizes the handling requirements for each classification level across key handling scenarios:

Storage (electronic) Any approved system Approved organizational systems only; no unapproved personal storage Approved organizational systems with access controls; encryption at rest recommended Approved organizational systems with strict access controls; encryption at rest required
Transmission (electronic) Any channel Internal channels preferred; standard email acceptable Encrypted channels required (TLS); avoid sending as unencrypted email attachment Encrypted channels required (TLS 1.2+); end-to-end encryption preferred; avoid email where possible
Printing Unrestricted Use secure printing where available; collect immediately from printer Secure printing required; collect immediately; do not leave unattended Secure printing required; collect immediately; log print activity; minimize printing

Sharing externally Permitted freely Requires manager authorization; use organizational email or approved file sharing Requires data owner authorization and NDA or data sharing agreement Requires data owner and senior management authorization; legally binding DPA required; log all disclosures

7. Data Retention and Disposal

All organizational information must be retained for the minimum period required to meet business, legal, regulatory, and contractual obligations, and disposed of securely once the retention period has expired. Retaining data beyond its required period unnecessarily increases risk and cost.

Retention schedules must be defined by data owners in consultation with Legal, Compliance, and the Information Security Manager, taking into account applicable legal and regulatory requirements. A Data Retention Schedule documenting retention periods for major data categories must be maintained and reviewed annually.

Disposal of data and the media on which it is stored must use methods appropriate to the classification level:

  • Public and Internal: Standard secure deletion (overwrite) for electronic data; cross-cut shredding for physical documents; standard equipment recycling is acceptable for hardware after data removal
  • Confidential: Multi-pass secure overwrite compliant with DoD 5220.22-M or equivalent for electronic media; cross-cut shredding for physical documents; hardware must be degaussed or overwritten before disposal or transfer
  • Restricted: Cryptographic erasure (rendering data unrecoverable by destroying encryption keys) or physical destruction (degaussing followed by shredding or disintegration) for electronic media in accordance with NIST SP 800-88 guidelines; micro-cut or cross-cut shredding for physical documents; a certificate of data destruction must be obtained and retained; third-party disposal vendors must be approved and must sign a data destruction agreement

The disposal date and method used must be recorded in the Asset Inventory (TMP-003).

8. Reclassification

Information may need to be reclassified if its content, context, or risk profile changes over time. For example, strategic plans may be reclassified from Restricted to Internal after a public announcement, or an internal document may require upgrade to Confidential if it is found to contain sensitive personal data.

The reclassification process:

  • Any user who believes information is misclassified may raise a reclassification request with the data owner
  • The data owner is responsible for reviewing the request and determining whether reclassification is appropriate
  • Reclassification decisions must be documented, including the reason for reclassification, the previous and new classification level, and the effective date
  • Upon reclassification, all copies of the information (electronic and physical) must be relabeled to reflect the new classification level, and handling practices must be adjusted accordingly
  • Reclassification to a lower level for Confidential or Restricted information requires approval from the Information Security Manager
  • Where there is a disagreement about the appropriate classification level, the Information Security Manager's determination is final
