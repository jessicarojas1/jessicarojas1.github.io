1. Purpose and Objective

This procedure defines the systematic, repeatable process for conducting information security risk assessments within the ISMS. Its purpose is to ensure that all information security risks are identified, analyzed, and evaluated consistently, providing management with the information needed to make informed decisions about risk treatment priorities.

The objectives of this procedure are to:

  • Establish a consistent methodology for identifying threats, vulnerabilities, and associated risks to information assets
  • Ensure risk assessments are conducted against defined criteria so that results are comparable and reproducible
  • Produce a Risk Register (TMP-002) that accurately reflects the organization's current risk exposure
  • Provide input to the risk treatment process (PRO-002) by identifying risks that require treatment
  • Satisfy the requirements of ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clauses 6.1.2 and 8.2

2. Scope

This procedure applies to all information assets within the ISMS scope as defined in the ISMS Scope Statement. It covers:

  • All information assets: hardware, software, data, services, people, and facilities
  • All threats applicable to those assets within and outside the defined ISMS boundary
  • All personnel involved in conducting, reviewing, or approving risk assessments
  • Annual scheduled risk assessments and triggered assessments arising from incidents, changes, or management direction

This procedure does not cover project-level or operational risk management, which are addressed by the organization's broader enterprise risk framework.

3. Definitions

TermDefinition
AssetAnything that has value to the organization and requires protection, including information, software, hardware, services, people, and intangibles such as reputation.
ThreatA potential cause of an unwanted incident that may result in harm to the organization or its information assets. Threats may be natural, accidental, or deliberate.
VulnerabilityA weakness of an asset or control that can be exploited by one or more threats, resulting in a security incident.
LikelihoodThe probability that a given threat will exploit a given vulnerability, expressed on a defined scale (1–5 in this procedure).
ImpactThe magnitude of harm resulting from the exploitation of a vulnerability, assessed across the dimensions of confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
Inherent RiskThe level of risk existing before the application of any controls or risk treatment measures.
Residual RiskThe level of risk remaining after risk treatment measures and existing controls have been applied.
Risk OwnerAn individual or entity with accountability and authority to manage an identified risk and the associated treatment actions.

4. Prerequisites

Before commencing a risk assessment, the following inputs must be available and current:

  1. Asset Inventory (TMP-003): A current and complete inventory of all in-scope information assets. If the inventory has not been updated within the past 6 months, it must be refreshed before proceeding.
  2. ISMS Scope Statement: The documented and approved scope of the ISMS, defining the boundaries and interfaces with external parties.
  3. Risk Criteria (POL-017): The organization's documented risk appetite, acceptance thresholds, and scoring criteria, approved by senior management. Risk criteria must be in place before risk evaluation can occur.
  4. Previous Risk Register (TMP-002): The most recent version of the Risk Register, to be used as a baseline for comparison and to carry forward risks that remain open.
  5. Threat Intelligence Inputs: Current threat intelligence feeds, vulnerability advisories, and information from sector-specific sources relevant to the organization's operating environment.

5. Procedure Steps

Step 1: Establish Risk Assessment Context

  1. Review the current ISMS Scope Statement and confirm no changes have occurred since the last assessment. If scope changes are identified, update the scope statement before proceeding and obtain management approval.
  2. Confirm the Asset Inventory (TMP-003) is current. Coordinate with asset owners and IT to verify all assets are listed. Flag any new systems, services, or data flows introduced since the last assessment for priority inclusion.
  3. Review the current threat landscape. Gather inputs from: threat intelligence subscriptions, national cyber security advisory bodies (e.g., CISA, NCSC), industry-specific ISACs, recent security incidents within the organization, and external audit or penetration testing findings.
  4. Confirm risk criteria with management. Review the risk appetite statement in POL-017. Confirm that the likelihood scale, impact scale, risk scoring formula, and risk acceptance threshold remain appropriate. Obtain written confirmation from the Information Security Manager that risk criteria are unchanged or document any approved changes.
  5. Convene the risk assessment team. Identify and brief all participants: the Information Security Manager (lead), asset owners, IT team representatives, and relevant subject matter experts. Document the team composition in the Risk Assessment Report.
  6. Schedule risk assessment sessions. For a full ISMS scope assessment, schedule a minimum of one workshop per asset category. Document the session schedule.

Step 2: Asset Identification

  1. Open the Asset Inventory (TMP-003) and review each asset entry. Confirm that all in-scope assets are listed and categorized using the standard asset categories: Information/Data, Software/Applications, Hardware/Infrastructure, Services, People/Roles, Facilities/Physical Infrastructure.
  2. For each asset, confirm or assign an Asset Owner. The Asset Owner is the individual or team accountable for the asset's security. No asset may proceed through the risk assessment without an assigned owner.
  3. For each asset, record: Asset Name, Asset Category, Asset Description, Data Classification (per POL-005), Physical/Logical Location, Asset Owner, and Dependencies (systems or services this asset depends on).
  4. Identify and document critical assets. Assets that, if compromised, would have significant impact on business operations, regulatory compliance, or stakeholder trust must be flagged as critical. Critical assets will receive priority attention during the threat and vulnerability identification steps.
  5. Update TMP-003 with any new assets identified. Obtain confirmation from Asset Owners that the inventory is accurate and complete. Record the date of confirmation.

Step 3: Threat Identification

  1. For each asset or asset group, identify all applicable threats. Use the organization's Threat Library as a starting point. The threat library includes, but is not limited to:
    • Malware and Ransomware: Malicious software designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access
    • Insider Threat: Malicious or negligent actions by current or former employees, contractors, or partners
    • Unauthorized Access: Exploitation of authentication weaknesses, stolen credentials, or misconfigured access controls
    • Social Engineering: Phishing, spear-phishing, vishing, and pretexting attacks targeting personnel
    • System Failure: Hardware failure, software bugs, database corruption, or application crashes
    • Natural Disaster: Flood, fire, earthquake, severe weather events affecting physical facilities
    • Supply Chain Attack: Compromise via third-party software, services, or hardware components
    • Denial of Service: Network or application-level attacks designed to disrupt availability
    • Data Exfiltration: Unauthorized copying or transfer of data to external parties
    • Physical Theft or Tampering: Theft of devices or physical media; tampering with hardware
    • Misconfiguration: Security weaknesses introduced by incorrect system or application configuration
    • Unpatched Vulnerabilities: Exploitation of known, unpatched software or firmware vulnerabilities
  2. For each threat, record: Threat Name, Threat Category (Deliberate / Accidental / Environmental), Threat Description, and Threat Source (external attacker, malicious insider, natural, etc.).
  3. Consider threat combinations where two or more simultaneous threats could amplify impact (e.g., a system failure occurring during an active security incident).
  4. Document identified threats in the Risk Register (TMP-002), linked to the relevant asset.

Step 4: Vulnerability Identification

  1. For each asset-threat pair, identify the specific vulnerabilities that could allow the threat to be realized. Gather vulnerability information from the following sources:
    • Automated vulnerability scan results (most recent scan, no older than 90 days)
    • Penetration testing reports (most recent engagement)
    • Internal and external audit findings
    • Personnel interviews with system administrators and users
    • Vendor security advisories and patch bulletins
    • Configuration review outputs
    • Incident post-mortems from previous security events
  2. For each vulnerability, record: Vulnerability Name/Description, the asset(s) affected, and the threat(s) it is associated with. Where possible, reference published vulnerability identifiers (e.g., CVE numbers).
  3. Classify each vulnerability by type: Technical (e.g., unpatched software), Process (e.g., no access review procedure), or People (e.g., insufficient security awareness training).
  4. Document all identified vulnerabilities in the Risk Register (TMP-002), linked to the relevant asset-threat pairs.

Step 5: Existing Controls Assessment

  1. For each asset-threat-vulnerability combination, identify and document all existing controls currently in place. Controls may be technical (firewalls, encryption, MFA), procedural (policies, training, change management), or physical (access card readers, CCTV, fire suppression).
  2. For each existing control, assess its effectiveness using the following rating: Effective (control operating as designed and fully mitigating the vulnerability), Partially Effective (control is in place but has identified weaknesses or gaps), Ineffective (control exists on paper but is not operating correctly or not consistently applied).
  3. Identify control gaps: vulnerabilities for which no control exists, or where existing controls are rated Partially Effective or Ineffective. Document each gap and its significance.
  4. Record all existing controls and their effectiveness ratings in the Risk Register (TMP-002). Control gaps will be prioritized for risk treatment in PRO-002.

Step 6: Likelihood Assessment

  1. For each risk (asset-threat-vulnerability combination), assess the likelihood that the threat will exploit the vulnerability, taking into account the effectiveness of existing controls. Use the following 5-point scale:
    RatingLabelDescriptionFrequency
    1RareThreat occurrence is exceptional; strong controls in place<1% probability per year
    2UnlikelyThreat has occurred in the sector but not expected here1–10% probability per year
    3PossibleRealistic possibility; some precedent in the organization or sector10–30% probability per year
    4LikelyExpected to occur in most circumstances; controls partially effective30–60% probability per year
    5Almost CertainExpected to occur; controls absent, ineffective, or already breached>60% probability per year
  2. Likelihood ratings must be justified in writing. The assessor must document the rationale, referencing threat intelligence, historical incident data, or control effectiveness evidence.
  3. Where there is disagreement among assessors, the Information Security Manager has the final determination on likelihood ratings. Dissenting views may be noted.

Step 7: Impact Assessment

  1. For each risk, assess the impact of the threat being realized against each of the three CIA dimensions:
    RatingLabelConfidentiality ImpactIntegrity ImpactAvailability Impact
    1NegligibleNo sensitive data disclosedNo data corruption; easily correctedOutage <1 hour; no user impact
    2MinorLimited internal data disclosed; no regulatory impactMinor data errors; detectable and correctableOutage 1–4 hours; limited user impact
    3ModerateInternal sensitive data disclosed; potential regulatory attentionModerate data corruption; significant correction effortOutage 4–24 hours; significant user and business impact
    4SignificantConfidential or personal data disclosed; regulatory breach likelyMajor data corruption; business process failureOutage 1–7 days; severe business and reputational impact
    5CatastrophicClassified or critical personal data disclosed at scale; severe regulatory and legal consequencesComplete data loss or destruction; unrecoverableExtended outage >7 days; existential threat to organization
  2. Assess impact across all three CIA dimensions. The overall impact rating for the risk is the highest rating across the three dimensions. Document all three individual ratings and the rationale for the overall impact rating.
  3. Consider cascading impacts: whether the compromise of this asset could lead to the compromise of other dependent assets or systems. Cascading impacts should increase the overall impact rating.

Step 8: Inherent Risk Calculation

  1. Calculate the Inherent Risk Score using the formula: Inherent Risk Score = Likelihood Rating × Impact Rating. This produces a score between 1 and 25.
  2. Map the calculated score to a risk level using the following matrix:
    Score RangeRisk LevelColor Code
    1–3Very LowGreen
    4–6LowBlue
    7–12MediumYellow/Amber
    13–19HighOrange
    20–25CriticalRed
  3. Record the Inherent Risk Score, Risk Level, and all component ratings in the Risk Register (TMP-002).

Step 9: Residual Risk Assessment

  1. Apply the existing controls identified in Step 5 to re-assess the likelihood and impact ratings. For each risk, re-rate likelihood and impact considering the mitigating effect of effective controls.
  2. Calculate the Residual Risk Score using the same formula: Residual Risk Score = Adjusted Likelihood × Adjusted Impact.
  3. If no existing controls are in place, or all controls are rated Ineffective, the Residual Risk Score is equal to the Inherent Risk Score.
  4. Record the Residual Likelihood Rating, Residual Impact Rating, Residual Risk Score, and Residual Risk Level in the Risk Register (TMP-002).
  5. Assign a Risk Owner to each identified risk. The Risk Owner is responsible for monitoring and managing the risk. The Risk Owner is typically the Asset Owner but may be a department head or business process owner depending on the nature of the risk.

Step 10: Risk Evaluation

  1. Compare each Residual Risk Score against the organization's risk acceptance threshold defined in POL-017. Risks at or below the acceptance threshold may be accepted. Risks above the acceptance threshold require treatment via PRO-002.
  2. Flag all risks that exceed the acceptance threshold as “Requires Treatment” in the Risk Register.
  3. For risks at or near the acceptance threshold, the Information Security Manager and relevant Risk Owner must make an explicit determination. The determination, including rationale, must be documented.
  4. Produce the Risk Evaluation Summary: a ranked list of risks requiring treatment, sorted by Residual Risk Score descending. This summary is a key input to PRO-002.
  5. Present the Risk Evaluation Summary to senior management for review and approval before proceeding to risk treatment.

7. Documentation Requirements

The following documentation must be produced and retained as evidence of the risk assessment:

  1. Completed Risk Register (TMP-002): Fully populated with all assessed risks, including asset, threat, vulnerability, existing controls, likelihood, impact, inherent risk, residual risk, risk level, and risk owner. The Risk Register is a controlled document updated after each assessment.
  2. Risk Assessment Report: A narrative report summarizing: scope and boundaries, assessment methodology, team composition, threat landscape considerations, key findings (top risks), changes from the previous assessment, and recommendations. The report must be approved and signed by the Information Security Manager.
  3. Evidence of Asset Owner Confirmation: Signed or email-evidenced confirmation from each Asset Owner that the asset inventory is accurate.
  4. Management Sign-Off: Documented approval from the Information Security Manager and, for risk assessments feeding into management review, from senior management. Sign-off records are retained in the ISMS document repository.
  5. Session Notes: Notes from risk assessment workshops documenting participants, discussions, and decisions. Retain as supporting evidence.

All risk assessment documentation must be retained for a minimum of 3 years in accordance with the Records Management requirements of PRO-003.

9. Quality Assurance

To ensure the quality, consistency, and integrity of risk assessments, the following quality assurance measures are applied:

  1. Peer Review: All risk assessments must be peer-reviewed by at least one individual other than the lead assessor. The peer reviewer must confirm that the methodology has been followed, ratings are justified, and documentation is complete.
  2. ISM Sign-Off: The Information Security Manager must review and formally approve the Risk Register and Risk Assessment Report before they are finalized and presented to management. The ISM's approval confirms that the assessment meets quality standards.
  3. Consistency Checks: Where multiple asset owners contribute to the assessment, the ISM must perform a consistency check to ensure that comparable assets, threats, and vulnerabilities have been rated on a consistent basis. Significant inconsistencies must be investigated and resolved before finalization.
  4. Year-on-Year Comparison: The Risk Assessment Report must include a comparison with the previous annual assessment, explaining material changes in risk scores, new risks identified, and risks that have been closed or reduced.
  5. Management Review: Risk assessment outputs are reviewed at the annual management review. Management's questions, comments, and decisions must be recorded and filed with the risk assessment documentation.
