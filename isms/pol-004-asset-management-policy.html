1. Purpose

This Asset Management Policy establishes the requirements for identifying, classifying, managing, and protecting information assets throughout their lifecycle. Effective asset management is foundational to information security: you cannot protect what you do not know you have. This policy ensures that all information assets are identified, assigned an owner, classified according to their sensitivity and criticality, and managed in accordance with their value to the organization.

This policy supports the organization's ISMS by providing the basis for risk assessment (all assets must be identified before their risks can be assessed), access control (assets must be classified before appropriate access restrictions can be applied), and incident response (accurate asset inventories enable faster incident scoping and response).

2. Scope

This policy applies to all information assets owned, leased, operated, or controlled by the organization, including assets managed by third parties on the organization's behalf. Information assets include, but are not limited to:

  • Hardware assets: Servers, workstations, laptops, mobile devices, network equipment (routers, switches, firewalls), storage devices, printers, and removable media
  • Software assets: Operating systems, applications, databases, firmware, development tools, and utilities
  • Data assets: Databases, data files, configuration data, backup data, audit logs, contracts, intellectual property, and any other structured or unstructured data
  • Service assets: Cloud services, SaaS applications, hosting services, communication services, and other IT services relied upon by the organization
  • People assets: Skills, expertise, and knowledge held by staff that are critical to organizational operations
  • Intangible assets: Reputation, brand, certifications, and intellectual property

This policy applies to all personnel responsible for managing, using, or overseeing organizational assets, and to all third parties managing assets on the organization's behalf.

3. Asset Inventory

The organization must maintain a complete, accurate, and up-to-date inventory of all information assets. The asset inventory is a foundational document for the ISMS and must be treated as a living record that reflects the current state of organizational assets at all times.

Requirements for the asset inventory:

  • All assets within scope must be registered in the organizational Asset Inventory (TMP-003) prior to being placed into operation or used to process organizational data
  • Each asset record must include at a minimum: unique asset identifier, asset name and description, asset type/category, asset owner, asset custodian (where applicable), location, classification level, acquisition date, support/warranty status, and disposal date
  • The asset inventory must be reviewed and validated at least annually, and updated immediately upon any change (acquisition, disposal, transfer, reassignment)
  • Automated asset discovery tools must be used where technically feasible to supplement manual inventory processes and detect unauthorized or unregistered assets
  • The Information Security Manager is responsible for maintaining the integrity of the asset inventory and coordinating periodic validation exercises
  • Discrepancies between the inventory and the actual asset population (unregistered assets, missing assets) must be investigated and resolved within 10 business days of discovery

4. Asset Ownership

Every information asset must have a designated owner. Asset ownership confers accountability for the security, appropriate use, and lifecycle management of the asset, and is distinct from physical custody or day-to-day management (custodianship).

Asset owner responsibilities include:

  • Ensuring the asset is registered in the asset inventory with accurate and current information
  • Assigning and maintaining the appropriate classification level for the asset in accordance with the Data Classification Policy (POL-005)
  • Approving access to the asset and ensuring that access rights are reviewed periodically in accordance with the Access Control Policy (POL-003)
  • Ensuring the asset is operated and maintained in accordance with applicable security policies, standards, and procedures
  • Ensuring appropriate controls are applied throughout the asset's lifecycle, including secure disposal at end of life
  • Notifying the Information Security Manager of any changes to the asset that may affect its classification, risk profile, or security controls

Asset ownership must be assigned to a named individual (not a team or role title alone) and must be updated promptly if the owner changes. Where an asset has no identified owner, the Information Security Manager assumes interim ownership until a permanent owner is assigned.

5. Acceptable Use of Assets

All information assets must be used in accordance with the Acceptable Use Policy (POL-002) and any asset-specific acceptable use guidelines established by the asset owner. Users are responsible for the appropriate use of assets assigned to them or made available for their use.

Users must not use organizational assets for purposes outside the scope of their authorized role without explicit approval from the asset owner and the Information Security Manager. Any misuse of assets — including unauthorized modification, unauthorized removal, or use for personal gain — will be treated as a policy violation subject to disciplinary action.

6. Asset Classification

All information assets must be classified according to their sensitivity, criticality, and the potential impact of their loss, disclosure, or modification. Classification determines the appropriate security controls to be applied to the asset throughout its lifecycle.

Asset classification must be performed in accordance with the Data Classification Policy (POL-005), which defines four classification levels: Public, Internal, Confidential, and Restricted. Asset owners are responsible for assigning the appropriate classification level to each asset under their ownership.

Classification must be reviewed whenever there is a material change to the asset's content, use, or risk profile. Assets must be reclassified when circumstances change, in accordance with the reclassification process defined in the Data Classification Policy (POL-005).

7. Asset Lifecycle Management

Assets must be managed securely throughout their entire lifecycle, from procurement through to disposal. Security must not be an afterthought applied only during operation; it must be embedded from the acquisition stage.

7.1 Procurement

All new hardware and software must be procured through an approved organizational process. Security requirements must be considered during the procurement evaluation and must be documented prior to acquisition. Suppliers must meet minimum security baseline requirements as defined in the Supplier and Third-Party Security Policy (POL-012). IT and Information Security must be consulted before any significant new asset type is procured.

7.2 Configuration and Deployment

All hardware and software assets must be configured in accordance with approved security configuration baselines before being placed into production. Default credentials must be changed, unnecessary services and ports must be disabled, and required security agents (antivirus, endpoint detection, MDM) must be installed and verified before the asset is made operational. Assets must be registered in the inventory at this stage.

7.3 Maintenance

Assets must be maintained throughout their operational life to preserve their security posture. This includes applying security patches and updates within timelines defined in the Patch Management Standard, performing regular backups, and conducting periodic security configuration reviews to ensure alignment with the current baseline. Hardware maintenance must be performed by authorized personnel or approved vendors only.

7.4 Disposal

Assets must be securely disposed of at end of life to prevent unauthorized recovery of organizational data. Prior to disposal, transfer, or repurposing of any asset:

  • All organizational data must be securely and irreversibly removed from the asset using methods appropriate to the asset's classification (see Data Classification Policy, POL-005, Section 7)
  • Software licenses must be deactivated and returned where applicable
  • The asset must be removed from the asset inventory and all associated records updated
  • Disposal must be documented, including the method of data destruction used, the disposal date, and the identity of the individual responsible
  • Third-party disposal vendors must provide a certificate of data destruction

8. Asset Return

All organizational assets assigned to or in the custody of an employee, contractor, or third party must be returned in good condition upon termination of employment or engagement, or upon request by management.

The return of assets must be coordinated as part of the offboarding process managed by Human Resources and IT. A checklist of assets assigned to the departing individual must be prepared and signed off at the time of return. Access to organizational systems must be revoked in accordance with the Access Control Policy (POL-003) at the same time as physical asset return is coordinated.

Failure to return organizational assets may result in deductions from final compensation to the extent permitted by applicable law, and/or legal action to recover the assets.

9. Software Asset Management

Software represents a significant category of organizational asset and carries distinct risks related to licensing compliance, security vulnerabilities, and unauthorized functionality.

  • Only software that has been approved by the IT department and is properly licensed may be installed on organizational systems. An approved software list must be maintained and communicated to all staff.
  • A software asset inventory must be maintained, recording all licensed software, version information, license type, license count, and expiry dates. This inventory must be reconciled against actual installations at least annually.
  • Unauthorized software — including freeware, shareware, browser extensions, peer-to-peer clients, or any software not on the approved list — must not be installed on organizational systems. Endpoint controls (application whitelisting or device management policies) must be used where technically feasible to prevent unauthorized software installation.
  • Software licenses must be managed to ensure compliance at all times. License over-use and under-use must both be identified and addressed. IT is responsible for tracking license consumption and renewing licenses before expiry.
  • End-of-life software (no longer supported by the vendor with security patches) must be identified, risk-assessed, and either upgraded, replaced, or isolated with compensating controls within a timeframe approved by the Information Security Manager.

10. Cloud and Virtual Assets

Cloud computing and virtualization introduce asset management challenges due to the dynamic and often ephemeral nature of cloud resources. The following requirements apply to cloud and virtual assets:

  • All cloud services (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS), cloud accounts, virtual machines, containers, and storage resources used by the organization must be inventoried in the asset inventory, including the responsible owner and classification of data processed
  • New cloud services and SaaS applications must be approved by IT and the Information Security Manager before being used for organizational data. Shadow IT (unsanctioned use of cloud services) is prohibited.
  • Cloud resources must be subject to the same classification, access control, and lifecycle management requirements as on-premises assets
  • Cloud asset inventories must be verified against actual cloud account resource usage at least quarterly, using cloud management or CSPM (Cloud Security Posture Management) tools where available
  • Unused or decommissioned cloud resources (virtual machines, storage buckets, databases) must be terminated promptly to reduce cost and attack surface, following the secure disposal requirements of Section 7.4
  • Data residency requirements for cloud-hosted data must be assessed and documented for all cloud services processing Confidential or Restricted data
